Prevalence of Antiretroviral Drug Resistance in HIV-1 Patients in Blantyre

By J. Phiri, L. Moyo, C. Banda

Abstract

Background: The scale-up of antiretroviral therapy (ART) in Malawi has been a major public health success. However, the emergence of HIV drug resistance (HIVDR) threatens its long-term effectiveness. This study aimed to determine the prevalence of HIVDR mutations in patients receiving long-term ART in Blantyre, Malawi.

Methods: A cross-sectional study was conducted among adult patients on first-line ART for at least 12 months. Plasma samples were collected for viral load testing and genotyping of the pol gene for those with virologic failure (viral load >1000 copies/mL). Resistance mutations were identified using the Stanford HIV Drug Resistance Database.

Results: Of the 450 participants, 52 (11.6%) had virologic failure. Genotyping was successful in 48 samples. Any form of HIVDR was detected in 40/48 (83.3%) of these samples. The most common mutations were M184V (75%) and K103N (60%), conferring resistance to lamivudine/emtricitabine and non-nucleoside reverse-transcriptase inhibitors (NNRTIs), respectively.

Conclusion: There is a high prevalence of HIVDR among patients with virologic failure on first-line ART in Blantyre. These findings underscore the need for enhanced viral load monitoring and access to second-line regimens to maintain the success of the national ART program.
